5. How many grams of calcium hydroxide are there in 230.0 mL of 2.8N solution?​


Answer: The answer is 0.644 x 74 = 47.656 grams of calcium hydroxide in 230.0 ml of 2.8N solution.

Explanation: To determine the number of grams of calcium hydroxide in 230.0 ml of 2.8N solution, we need to use the formula: Molarity = moles of solute/liters of solution. Rearranging the formula to solve for moles of solute, we get moles of solute = Molarity x liters of solution:

We need to convert 230.0 ml to liters by dividing by 1000, which gives 0.23 L.We need to calculate the moles of calcium hydroxide using the molarity of 2.8N. 2.8N means 2.8 moles of calcium hydroxide per liter of solution. Therefore, moles of calcium hydroxide = 2.8 x 0.23 = 0.644 moles.We can calculate the grams of calcium hydroxide by multiplying the moles by the molar mass of calcium hydroxide, which is 74 g/mol.

20. what is the weight percent of calcium hydroxide in a solution that is made by dissolving 8.35g of Ca (OH)2 in 52g of H2O?​


Percent by massDiscussion:

Percent by mass (also called percent by weight or weight percent) is the ratio of the mass of a solute to the mass of the solution, multiplied by 100. The formulas used in solving percent by mass are as follows:

[tex]\boxed{\text{percent by mass} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solution}} × 100}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{percent by mass} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solute + mass of solvent}} × 100}[/tex]

The percent by mass is a unitless number because it is a ratio of two similar quantities.

[tex]\\[/tex]

Problem:

What is the weight percent of calcium hydroxide in a solution that is made by dissolving 8.35 g of Ca(OH)2 in 52.0 g of H2O?

Given:

[tex]\text{mass of solute = 8.35 g}[/tex]

[tex]\text{mass of solvent = 52.0 g}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solute} = \text{Ca(OH)}_{2}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solvent} = \text{H}_{2}\text{O}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]\text{weight percent}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]\text{weight percent} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solute + mass of solvent}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{weight percent} = \frac{\text{8.35 g}}{\text{8.35 g + 52.0 g}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{weight percent} = 13.8\%}[/tex]

24. How many moles of calcium hydroxide must be present in 0.250 L solution to give a pH of 9.28?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate the pOH.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{pOH} & = 14 - \text{pH} \\ & = 14 - 9.28 \\ & = 4.72 \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the OH⁻ concentration.

[tex]\begin{aligned} [\text{OH}^-] & = 10^{-\text{pOH}} \\ & = 10^{-4.72} \\ & = 1.90546 \times 10^{-5} \: M \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Determine the initial concentration of Ca(OH)₂.

Since Ca(OH)₂ has two OH⁻ ions, the initial concentration is equal to half of the equilibrium concentration of OH⁻.

[tex]\begin{aligned} [\text{Ca(OH)}_2]_{i} & = \frac{1}{2}[\text{OH}^-] \\ & = \frac{1}{2}(1.90546 \times 10^{-5} \: M) \\ & = 9.5273 \times 10^{-6} \: M \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the number of moles of Ca(OH)₂.

Note: 1 M = 1 mol/L

[tex]\begin{aligned} n & = ([\text{Ca(OH)}_2]_{i})(V_{\text{solution}}) \\ & = (9.5273 \times 10^{-6} \: \text{mol/L})(\text{0.250 L}) \\ & = \boxed{2.38 \times 10^{-6} \: \text{mol}}\end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 2.38 × 10⁻⁶ mol of Ca(OH)₂ must be present to give a pH of 9.28.

[tex]\small\sf{Molar \: mass \: of \: Ca(OH)_{2} = 40.08 + 2(16.00) + 2(1.01)}[/tex]
