22. what would be like to be in the shoes of a chinese, japanese, or korean composer?​


I speak Chinese and am also learning Korean.

From an objective perspective, Chinese is probably the hardest language to learn, because of many reasons.

Tones in Chinese can be incredibly hard. Mandarin Chinese has four tones, but Cantonese can have eight. The meaning of a word changes completely if the tone is changed. On top of that, there are many different meanings with one tone. For example là. This can mean 辣, which means spice or spicy. It can also mean 蠟, which means wax. It can also mean 落, which in some instances mean to miss. Almost every sound imaginable has several meanings, so in speech it is up to the context.

Chinese also have different meaning for the same character. Let’s take 的. This can be pronounced dē, and is mostly used as a possessive marker in Chinese. Now if you write 的確, which means indeed, it is pronounced as dí. If you write 打的, which means to call a taxi, it is pronounced as dī. If you write 目的, which means goal, it is pronounced as di (third sound. Quora doesn’t accept the thing we use to indicate the third sound).

In Chinese there are as much 50,000 characters, but you’ll only need 2,000–3,000 to read a newspaper. That’s still a lot of characters before you’ll be able to read basic things.

But for grammar, Chinese is much easier than English. We don’t have tenses in Chinese. You simply just say when you did it and people will know. We also don’t have different forms of a verb depending on different pronouns. We simply just use the verb behind.

Chinese sentences also comes in the form subject-verb-object, in contrary to Korean and Japanese where sentences comes in subject-object-verb. This basically means that they say “The dog food eat”. This can be more confusing for people that grew up using subject-verb-object.

Second after Chinese the hardest language I would say is Japanese. I don’t know that much about Japanese, but I had friends that learned Japanese, so I did some research just to understand what they were talking about. Also, I don’t know how to type Japanese. So here we go.

Japanese has three parts or components in their system. Kanji, hiragana, and katakana. I have no idea what the differences of these three are, except that kanji is adopted from Chinese characters, which makes it possible for Chinese people to read and kind of understand the context of Japanese writing.

If I’m not wrong, Japanese has different pronunciations for vowels and consonants. This is confusing.

As for Korean, they have an alphabet, which is called 한글 (han-geul).

The alphabet makes Korean much easier to learn, because you can read anything after learning it and a few other rules like ending consonants and such. Really much less after taking in perspective the 3000 characters you need to learn for Chinese, am I right?

Korean has more complicated grammar structure than Chinese, especially for certain words like to be “이다” in Korean, which is treated neither like a verb nor an adjective.

Different degrees of formality can also be a little confusing in the beginning. If I was to say “I am a student” I would say it different in different situations.

나는 학생이야 (informal)

저는 학생이에요 (formal)

I don’t know enough about Korean yet to explain anything more without confusing myself. Still learning!

In my own opinion, Japanese is the hardest one because it’s the one language that I am unfamiliar with.

But all of these languages have difficult and relatively easier parts. Any language can be learned through patience, hard work, and persistence.

Good luck if you’re learning any languages!
