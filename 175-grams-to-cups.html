2. If 25 grams of NaOH is dissolved in 175 grams of water, the percentage solute is equal to?


✏️PERCENT BY MASS

[tex]\tt{\huge{\green{Solution:}}}[/tex]

The problem asks to calculate for the percent by mass of solute. First, we need to identify the given values. These are

(Note: NaOH is the solute and water is the solvent)

mass of solute = 25 g

mass of solvent = 175 g

Finally, we can now calculate the percent by mass of the solute. Therefore, the percent by mass is

[tex]\text{percent by mass} = \dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solute + mass of solvent}} \times 100\%[/tex]

[tex]\text{percent by mass} = \dfrac{\text{25 g}}{\text{25 g + 175 g}} \times 100\%[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{percent by mass} = 12.5\%}[/tex]

19. A dilute sulfuric acid (H2SO4) solution is being prepared by the workers of an oil refinery. They had 105 grams of sulfuric acid used to prepare 175 L of solution. What is the molar concentration or molarity of the solution they are preparing? solve this problem.​


Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of solute (H₂SO₄).

molar mass = (1.008 g/mol × 2) + (32.07 g/mol × 1) + (16.00 g/mol × 4)

molar mass = 98.086 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\text{moles of solute = 105 g H₂SO₄} × \frac{\text{1 mol H₂SO₄}}{\text{98.086 g H₂SO₄}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute = 1.0705 mol}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molarity of solution.

[tex]\text{molarity} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{volume of solution (L)}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molarity} = \frac{\text{1.0705 mol}}{\text{175 L}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{molarity = 0.00612 M}}[/tex]

22. calculate the molecular weight of a 5 gram nonvolatile nonelectrolyte substance in 175 g chloroform with a boiling point of 62.2°C. problem solving


Given:

mass of solute = 5 g

mass of solvent = 175 g = 0.175 kg

[tex]\text{BP}_{solution} = \text{62.2°C}[/tex]

solute: non-volatile non-electrolyte

solvent: chloroform

Required:

molecular weight of solute

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the boiling point elevation.

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = \text{BP}_{solution} - \text{BP}_{solvent}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = \text{62.2°C} - \text{61.2°C}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = \text{1.0°C}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molality of the solution.

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = K_{\text{b}}m[/tex]

[tex]m = \frac{\Delta T_{\text{b}}}{k_{\text{b}}}[/tex]

[tex]m = \frac{\text{1.0°C}}{\text{3.62 °C/}m}[/tex]

[tex]m = 0.27624 \: m[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = m × \text{mass of solvent}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = 0.27624 \: m × \text{0.175 kg}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute = 0.048342 mol}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the molecular weight of the solute.

[tex]\text{molecular weight of solute} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{moles of solute}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molecular weight of solute} = \frac{\text{5 g}}{\text{0.048342 mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{molecular weight of solute = 103 g/mol}}[/tex]

28. A solution is prepared by dissolving 4.9 grams of sucrose (C12H220,1) in 175 grams water. Calculate the boiling point of this solution.​


Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of solute (sucrose).

molar mass = (12.01 g/mol × 12) + (1.008 g/mol × 22) + (16.00 g/mol × 11)

molar mass = 342.296 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \text{4.9 g sucrose} × \frac{\text{1 mol sucrose}}{\text{342.296 g sucrose}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \text{0.0143151 mol}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molality of the solution.

[tex]m = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent (kg)}}[/tex]

[tex]m = \frac{\text{0.0143151 mol}}{\text{0.175 kg}}[/tex]

[tex]m = 0.0818006 \: m[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the boiling point elevation.

[tex]∆T_{b} = mk_{b}[/tex]

[tex]∆T_{b} = (0.0818006 \: m)(\text{0.52 °C/}m)[/tex]

[tex]∆T_{b} = \text{0.0425°C}[/tex]

Step 5: Calculate the boiling point of the solution.

[tex]T_{b} = T°_{b} + ∆T_{b}[/tex]

[tex]T_{b} = \text{100°C + 0.0425°C}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{T_{b} = \text{100.0425°C}}[/tex]

30. calculate the molecular weight of a 5 gram nonvolatile noneletrolyte substance in 175 chloroform with a boiling point of 62.2 degree c​


The molecular weight of nonvolatile noneletrolyte substance : 110 g/mol

Further explanation  

Solution properties are the properties of a solution that don't depend on the type of solute but only on the concentration of the solute.  

Solution properties of electrolyte solutions differ from non-electrolyte solutions because electrolyte solutions contain a greater number of particles because electrolytes break down into ions. So the Solution properties of electrolytes are greater than non-electrolytes.  

The term is used in the Solution properties  

1. molal  

that is, the number of moles of solute in 1 kg of solvent  

[tex]\large {\boxed {\bold {m = mole. \dfrac {1000} {mass \: of \: solvent (in \: grams)}}}[/tex]

2. Boiling point  

Solutions from non-volatile substances have a higher boiling point and lower freezing points than the solvent  

ΔTb = Tb solution - Tb solvent  

ΔTb = boiling point elevation  

[tex]\large {\boxed {\boxed {\bold {\Delta Tb \: = \: Kb.m}}}[/tex]

Kb = molal boiling point increase  

m = molal solution  

We complete the data :

The boiling point of pure chloroform is 61.2 °C. The boiling point constant for chloroform is 3.85 °C/m

ΔTb = 62.2°C-61.2°C = 1°C

then molality of solution :

[tex]\rm m=\dfrac{\Delta Tb}{Kb}\\\\m=\dfrac{1}{3.85}[/tex]

molal = the number of moles of solute in 1 kg of solvent

So :

[tex]\rm m=\dfrac{mol~of~non~volatile~nonelectrolyte}{kg~solvent(chloroform)}\\\\m=\dfrac{mass/molecular~weight}{kg~solvent}\\\\m=\dfrac{5/Mw}{0.175~kg}\\\\\dfrac{1}{3.85}=\dfrac{5/Mw}{0.175}\\\\\dfrac{5\times 3.85}{Mw}=0.175\\\\\dfrac{19.25}{Mw}=0.175\Rightarrow Mw=\boxed{\bold{110~g/mol}}[/tex]
