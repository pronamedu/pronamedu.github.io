Conduction transfers heat via direct molecular collision. An area of greater kinetic energy will transfer thermal energy to an area with lower kinetic energy. Higher-speed particles will collide with slower speed particles. The slower-speed particles will increase in kinetic energy as a result. Convection

When a fluid, such as air or a liquid, is heated and then travels away from the source, it carries the thermal energy along. This type of heat transfer is called convection. The fluid above a hot surface expands, becomes less dense, and rises.

Radiation

Thermal radiation generates from the emission of electromagnetic waves. These waves carry the energy away from the emitting object. Radiation occurs through a vacuum or any transparent medium (either solid or fluid). Thermal radiation is the direct result of random movements of atoms and molecules in matter. Answer:

Thermal energy and internal energy are related concepts, but they are not exactly the same thing.

Explanation:

Thermal energy refers to the total energy of the particles that make up an object or a system. It includes the kinetic energy of the particles due to their motion and the potential energy due to their position relative to each other. Thermal energy is proportional to the temperature of the object or system.

Internal energy, on the other hand, refers to the total energy of the particles within a system that are associated with the motion and configuration of those particles. This includes the thermal energy of the particles, but it also includes other forms of energy such as chemical energy, nuclear energy, and potential energy due to the position of the particles within the system.

In summary, thermal energy is a component of internal energy, but internal energy includes additional forms of energy beyond thermal energy.

26. 1. The fact that a thermometer takes its own temperature illustrates_________.A. thermal equilibriumB. energy conservationC. that molecules are constantly movingD. the difference between heat and thermal energy ​


The fact that a thermometer takes its own temperature illustrates thermal equilibrium. A thermometer records the temperature it is in. We can say that a thermometer monitors the temperature of something else and that two items are at the same temperature by using the notions of thermal equilibrium and the zeroth law of thermodynamics.

What is thermal equilibrium?

If there is no net thermal energy transfer between any two physical systems connected by a heat-permeable channel, they are said to be in thermal equilibrium. Thermal equilibrium results in the application of the zeroth law of thermodynamics. A system is said to be in thermal equilibrium with itself if the temperature inside of it is constant across time and uniformly distributed.

Thermal equilibrium always exists in systems that are in thermodynamic equilibrium, while the reverse is not always true. The two systems may reach thermal equilibrium without reaching thermodynamic equilibrium if the link between them permits the transmission of energy as a "change in internal energy" but forbids the transfer of matter or energy as work.

Heat is the energy transfer from a high temperature to a low temperature. When these temperatures match and the heat flow stops, the system (or group of systems) is said to be in thermal equilibrium. Another result of thermal equilibrium is the absence of material flowing into or out of the system. The zeroth law of thermodynamics uses the idea of thermal equilibrium to explain how two different systems can be said to be at the same temperature.
