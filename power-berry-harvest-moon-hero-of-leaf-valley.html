22. It refers to the earth-like that has a molten heavy-metal core, few moons and topological features such as valleys, volcanoes and craters.


TERRESTRIAL PLANETS

Terrestrial planets are Earth-like planets composed of a surface of rocks or metals. Additionally, terrestrial planets have a molten core of heavy metal, few moons, and geological features like valleys, volcanoes, and craters.

Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars are referred to as terrestrial because their surfaces are rocky and compact, similar to Earth's terra firma. Terrestrial planets are the solar system's four innermost planets.

Terrestrial planets include the four innermost planets, which feature a liquid heavy-metal core, at least one moon, valleys, volcanoes, and craters.

Collisions between planetary embryos from the inner solar system or the accumulation of millimeter-sized, sunward-drifting "pebbles" from the outer solar system led to their formation.

While terrestrial planets formed from planetesimals composed of rocks and metals, they were too tiny to absorb significant amounts of the plentiful hydrogen and helium in the solar nebula. However, the jovian planets originated distant from the Sun, where ices and minerals were abundant.
