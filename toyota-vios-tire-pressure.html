4. a tireis filled to a gauge pressure of 2 atm at 27 C after a drive the temperature within the tire rises to 47 Cwhat is the pressure within the tire now​


Answer:

2.13atm

The following information were obtained from the question:

P1 (initial pressure) = 2atm

T1 (initial temperature) = 27°C = 27 + 273 = 300K

T2 (final temperature) = 47°C = 47 + 273 = 320K

P2 (final pressure) =?

Using the equation P1/T1 = P2/T2, the final pressure within the tyre can be obtained as illustrated below:

P1/T1 = P2/T2

2/300 = P2/320

Cross multiply to express in linear form

300 x P2 = 2 x 320

Divide both side by 300

P2 = (2 x 320)/300

P2 = 2.13atm

Therefore, the pressure within the tyre at 47°C is 2.13atm

6. The front tire pressure was measure to be 240 kPa at 28°C. What would be the front tire pressure if the temperature increased to34°C, assuming that no volume change occurred?​


Given:

[tex]P_{1} = \text{240 kPa}[/tex]

[tex]T_{1} = \text{28°C + 273 = 301 K}[/tex]

[tex]T_{2} = \text{34°C + 273 = 307 K}[/tex]

Unknown:

[tex]P_{2}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]\frac{P_{1}}{T_{1}} = \frac{P_{2}}{T_{2}}[/tex]

[tex]P_{2} = P_{1} × \frac{T_{2}}{T_{1}}[/tex]

[tex]P_{2} = \text{240 kPa} × \frac{\text{307 K}}{\text{301 K}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{P_{2} = \text{245 kPa}}[/tex]

10. ways of calculating tire pressure​


How to Calculate Correct Tire Pressure

Read the manufacturer's maximum inflation pressure on the side of the tire.

Calculate 10 percent of this amount. If, for example, the maximum is 40 psi, then 10 percent of 40 is 4. Subtract the 10 percent value from the maximum psi value. So, 40 - 4 = 36. Use 36 psi in your tires.

The pressure in an automobile tire is 1.88 atm at 25.0 degree C what will be the pressure if the temperature warms up to 37.0 degree ?

We can assume that it is ideal or perfect gas and we can use the formula from Gay-Lussac's relating the pressure and temperature of a gas.

Given: initial pressure (P1)= 1.88 atm

        Initial temperature (T1)= 25.0 °C  ⇒convert to absolute temperature (K)

                                         T1= 25.0 +273.15 K=298.15K

       Final temperature (T2)= 37.0 °C  ⇒convert to absolute temperature (K)

                                        T2= 37+273.15= 310.15K

Required: Final Pressure (P2)

Solution:

P1 / T1 = P2 / T2

P2 = (P1 × T2)/ T1

P2= (1.88 atm × 310.15 K) / (298.15 K)

P2= 1.96 atm

We can see that as the temperature increases, the pressure will also increase. Therefore, the pressure and temperature of an ideal gas (at constant volume (isochoric) and constant number of moles ) is directly proportional.                                                                                                                            


24. A motorist equips her automobile tires with a relief– type valve so that the pressure inside the tire never willexceed 240 kPa (gage). She stars a trip with a pressure of 200 kPa(gage) and a temperature of 23o C in the tires. Duringthe long drive the temperature of the air in the tires reaches80o C. Each tire contains 0.11kg of air. Determine (a)the mass of air escaping each tire; (b) the pressure of the tirewhen the temperature returns to 23oC.


We treat the air in the tires as an ideal gas and solve the missing values accordingly.

Ideal gas law:

[tex] PV = nRT [/tex]

Values:

[tex] P_{max} = 240 kPa[/tex]
[tex] P_{i} = 200 kPa[/tex]

these two pressures are gauge pressures, which means the actual pressure is benchmarked to 1 atm, which is 101.325 kPa. For consistency, we add that to our given pressures so that we solve for the actual pressure.

[tex] P_{max} = 240 kPa + 101.325 kPa = 341.325 kPa[/tex]
[tex] P_{i} = 200 kPa + 101.325 kPa = 301.325 kPa [/tex]

We also convert the temperatures to Kelvin, since this is the basic unit for ideal gases:

[tex] T_{i} = 23 \textdegree C = 296 K[/tex]
[tex] T_{max} = 80 \textdegree C = 353 K[/tex]
[tex] m_{i} = 0.11 kg[/tex]
[tex] R_{air} = 0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K}[/tex],

since we're using this constant, it's safe to change the n in the equation to m, as in mass.

First thing we need to do is solve for the volume of the tires initially. We're gonna need this since this is going to be a useful constant throughout the solution. Again, ideal gas law's gonna be perfect for it:

[tex] PV = mRT[/tex], we need volume so we twist it around to get a solution for volume

[tex] V = \frac{mRT}{P} [/tex],

We plug in the values at the start of the trip to solve this:

[tex] V = \frac{m_i R_{air}T_i}{P_i} [/tex]
[tex] V = \frac{(0.11 kg)(0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K})(296 K)}{301.325 kPa}[/tex]
[tex] V= \frac{(0.11 kg)(0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K})(296 K)}{301.325 \frac{kJ}{m^3}}[/tex]
[tex] V = 0.03102 m^3 [/tex]

For a)

We plug in this V to get the mass at the point of the trip where her tires got 240 kPA at 80 °C:

[tex] m = \frac{P_{max} V}{R_{air} T_{max}} [/tex]
[tex] m = \frac{(341.325 kPa)(0.03102 m^3)}{(0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K})(353 K)} [/tex]
[tex] m = \frac{(341.325 \frac{kJ}{m^3})(0.03102 m^3)}{(0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K})(353 K)}[/tex]
[tex] m = m_f = 0.10448 kg [/tex]

We subtract this mass to the initial mass to get the actual mass loss in the tires:

[tex] \Delta m = m_i - m_f = 0.11 kg - 0.10448 kg = 0.005517 kg [/tex]

∴ the mass loss in the tires is 0.005517 kg

For b)

We apply the final mass to solve for the pressure after the relief:

[tex]P = \frac{m_f R_{air} T}{V} [/tex]
[tex]P = \frac{m_f R_{air} T}{V} [/tex]
[tex]P = \frac{(0.10448 kg) (0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K}) (296 K)}{0.03102 m^3} [/tex]

∴ P = 286.20083 kPa (actual pressure), 184.876 kPa (gauge pressure)

29. the pressure in a car tire is 198 kPa at 27.after a long drive, the pressure is 225kPa what is the temperature of the air in the tire?


Answer:

340.9°k ~ 67.8°C

Explanation:

This is an example of Gay-Lussac's law, which states that the pressure of a given amount of gas held at constant volume is directly proportional to the Kelvin temperature. This means that if the volume increases, so does the temperature, and vice versa.

The equation for this gas law is:

P1/T1 = P2/T2

Known

P1 = 198 kPa

T1 = 27°C + 273.15 = 300°K ← Temp. must be in Kelvins

P2 = 225 kPa

T2 = ?

Solution

Rearrange the equation to isolate T2

Input the known values and solve.

T2 = T1P2/P1

Where:

T2 = 300°K × 225kPa/198kPa = 340.91°K

T2 in °C = 340.91°K − 273.15 = 67.8°C

note: answers were rounded off to one decimal point.
