2. 1. Calculate the mole fraction of sulphuric acid (H2SO4) in 8% (w/w0 aqueous H2SO4 solution. (molar masses: H2SO4 = 98 g/mol; H2O = 18 g/mol)​


The mole fraction of Sulphuric acid H₂SO₄ : = 0.016

Further explanation  

The concentration of a substance can be expressed in several quantities such as moles, percent (%) weight/volume,), molarity, molality, parts per million (ppm), or mole fraction.  

Percent mass of the component shows the total mass of the component in the overall mass of a compound/mixture  

[tex]\tt \%mass=\dfrac{mass~solute}{mass~solution}\times 100\%[/tex]

Solution = Solute + Solvent  

The mole fraction shows the mole ratio of a substance to the mole of solution /mixture  

Can be formulated :

[tex]\tt xa=\dfrac{na}{na+nb}[/tex]  

xa + xb = 1

Given

8% (w/w) aqueous  H₂SO₄ solution

Required

the mole fraction

Solution

For 100 g solution :

H₂SO₄ = 8% x 100 g = 8 g

H₂O = 92% x 100 g = 92 g

mol H₂SO₄ :

= mass : molar mass

= 8 g : 98 g/mol

= 0.082

mol H₂O :

= 92 g : 18 g/mol

= 5.1

total mol :

= 0.082 + 5.1

= 5.182

mol fraction of H₂SO₄ :

= 0.082 : 5.182

= 0.016

24. Answer:

21.2 kJ/mol

Explanation:

Let's consider the following reaction for the synthesis of nitric acid.

NaNO₃(s) + H₂SO₄(l) → NaHSO₄(s) + HNO₃(g)

We can calculate the standard enthalpy of the reaction (ΔH°rxn) using the following expression.

ΔH°rxn = ∑ np × ΔH°f(p) - ∑ nr × ΔH°f(r)

where,

n: moles

ΔH°f: standard enthalpy of formation

p: products

r: reactants

ΔH°rxn = 1 mol × ΔH°f(NaHSO₄) + 1 mol × ΔH°f(HNO₃) - 1 mol × ΔH°f(NaNO₃) - 1 mol × ΔH°f(H₂SO₄)

ΔH°rxn = 1 mol × (-1125.5 kJ/mol) + 1 mol × (-135.1 kJ/mol) - 1 mol × (-467.8 kJ/mol) - 1 mol × (-814.0 kJ/mol)

27. 3.) A student from section Kaaya-aya of Parang High School mixed 0.20 mol of Fe with 0.25 mol of H2SO4 to produce FeSO4 and H2 gas according to the following balanced equation: Fe + H2SO4 ---> FeSO4 + H2. A. Which is the limiting reactant, and which is the excess reactant?B. What amount of excess reactant will remain unreacted at the end of the reaction?​


Answer:

A. To determine the limiting reactant, we need to compare the moles of Fe and H2SO4 used in the reaction.

Using the balanced equation: 1 mol Fe reacts with 1 mol H2SO4.

The student used 0.20 mol of Fe and 0.25 mol of H2SO4.

Since the stoichiometry of the reaction is 1:1, we can see that H2SO4 is the limiting reactant because there isn't enough of it to react with all of the Fe. Thus, Fe is the excess reactant.

B. To determine the amount of excess reactant that remains unreacted, we need to calculate how much Fe is left over.

From the balanced equation, we know that 1 mol Fe reacts with 1 mol H2SO4 to produce 1 mol H2. Therefore, since all the limiting reactant is consumed, we can assume that 0.20 mol of Fe reacted to produce 0.20 mol of H2.

To find the amount of excess Fe, we need to calculate how much Fe would react with 0.25 mol of H2SO4. Using the mole ratio, we get:

0.25 mol H2SO4 x (1 mol Fe/1 mol H2SO4) = 0.25 mol Fe needed

Since only 0.20 mol of Fe was used, it means that there is an excess of:

0.25 mol Fe needed - 0.20 mol Fe used = 0.05 mol Fe excess

Therefore, 0.05 mol of Fe will remain unreacted at the end of the reaction.
