5. what is editorial article? example


Answer:

An editorial, leading article (US) or leader (UK), is an article written by the senior editorial people or publisher of a newspaper, magazine, or any other written document, often unsigned. Australian and major United States newspapers, such as The New York Times[1] and The Boston Globe,[2] often classify editorials under the heading "opinion".

Explanation:

Illustrated editorials may appear in the form of editorial cartoons.[3]

Typically, a newspaper's editorial board evaluates which issues are important for their readership to know the newspaper's opinion on.[4]

Editorials are typically published on a dedicated page, called the editorial page, which often features letters to the editor from members of the public; the page opposite this page is called the op-ed page and frequently contains opinion pieces (hence the name think pieces) by writers not directly affiliated with the publication. However, a newspaper may choose to publish an editorial on the front page. In the English-language press, this occurs rarely and only on topics considered especially important; it is more common, however, in some European countries such as Denmark, Spain, Italy, and France.[5]

Many newspapers publish their editorials without the name of the leader writer. Tom Clark, leader-writer for The Guardian, says that it ensures readers discuss the issue at hand rather than the author.[6] On the other hand, an editorial does reflect the position of a newspaper and the head of the newspaper, the editor, is known by name. Whilst the editor will often not write the editorial themselves, they maintain oversight and retain responsibility.[7]

In the field of fashion publishing, the term is often used to refer to photo-editorials – features with often full-page photographs on a particular theme, designer, model or other single topic, with or (as in a photo-essay) without accompanying text.[8]

6. 7 types of editorial and examples​


Editorials are written pieces of opinion that appear in newspapers, magazines, or websites. The following are seven types of editorials, along with examples:

1. Persuasive Editorials: These editorials aim to persuade readers to take a particular action or to hold a particular opinion. For example, an editorial that argues for the need to pass stricter gun control laws might be considered a persuasive editorial.
2. Explanatory Editorials: These editorials aim to explain complex issues or concepts to readers. For example, an editorial that explains the science behind climate change might be considered an explanatory editorial.
3. Interpretive Editorials: These editorials aim to provide interpretation or analysis of a particular event or issue. For example, an editorial that analyzes the impact of a new policy proposal might be considered an interpretive editorial.
4. Criticizing Editorials: These editorials aim to criticize or condemn certain actions, policies or behaviors. For example, an editorial that criticizes a politician for being dishonest might be considered a criticizing editorial.
5. Commemorative Editorials: These editorials aim to commemorate a particular event or person. For example, an editorial that commemorates the life and legacy of a civil rights leader might be considered a commemorative editorial.
6. Advocacy Editorials: These editorials aim to advocate for a particular group or cause. For example, an editorial that advocates for greater funding for public education might be considered an advocacy editorial.
7. Humorous Editorials: These editorials aim to be funny or satirical in nature. For example, an editorial that uses humor to criticize a government policy might be considered a humorous editorial.

18. 5 example of editorial paragraph​


Example 1:

The recent decision by the Supreme Court to overturn Roe v. Wade is a devastating blow to women's rights. This decision will have a profound impact on the lives of millions of women across the country, and it will make it much more difficult for women to access safe and legal abortion care. We must stand up and fight for women's rights, and we must make sure that this decision is not allowed to stand.

Example 2:

The ongoing war in Ukraine is a tragedy for the Ukrainian people and for the world. The Russian invasion has caused widespread death and destruction, and it has displaced millions of people. We must do everything we can to help the people of Ukraine, and we must work to bring an end to this war as soon as possible.

Example 3:

The climate crisis is a real and present danger. The evidence is clear: the Earth is warming, and the consequences are already being felt around the world. We must take action now to address this crisis, or we will face even more severe consequences in the future. We must transition to clean energy, we must invest in climate adaptation, and we must work to reduce our emissions.

Example 4:

The COVID-19 pandemic is still a threat, and we must continue to take precautions to protect ourselves and our communities. We must get vaccinated, we must wear masks, and we must social distance. We must also support our healthcare workers and make sure that everyone has access to the care they need.

Example 5:

The rise of misinformation and disinformation is a serious threat to our democracy. We must be vigilant in identifying and countering false information, and we must support fact-checking organizations. We must also teach our children how to critically evaluate information and how to spot fake news.

21. Give one example of editorial


Editorial: Bullying: Students themselves may be key to solving problem

Anyone who was bullied as a student – which includes most of us – remembers that the best defense was always to avoid the bully, if possible. If you took a different way to class, skipped lunch or darted for the door the second the bell rang, you might just survive another day without an embarrassing confrontation.

It used to be that bullies were creatures of opportunity. If they came across a potential victim, they would act, especially if they had an audience. They didn’t necessarily seek out their victims.

That’s how students who went to school before the age of the Internet and social media dealt with bullies. But things have changed and those who dismiss the issue today, those who say, “I was bullied in school and I turned out OK,” don’t understand how the world has changed for young people.

In the age of ubiquitous electronic communication, the bully cannot be avoided. There is no place to hide, no alternate routes to take, no way to stay out of the bully’s way. In short, no relief.

Social media has left bullying victims with few ways to escape, Andy Hagler, executive director of the Mental Health Association in Forsyth County, told the Journal’s Arika Herron. And perhaps because of the nature of social media, the bullying can be more extreme, leading some students to commit suicide. Gay and lesbian students are often specifically targeted.

"Nowadays with bullying, it’s more than just the teasing,” Hagler said. “It has a lot to do with intimidation. It’s not just a rite of passage. It becomes fear-based.”

October is National Bullying Prevention month. Winston-Salem/Forsyth County school officials are working with community groups to raise awareness about bullying and cyber-bullying. A survey of students last year revealed that 53 percent of eighth-graders, 43 percent of fifth-graders, and 28 percent of high-school seniors agreed or strongly agreed that bullying was a problem.

Last Wednesday was Unity Day, sponsored by the PACER National Bullying Prevention Center, an effort to raise awareness and show support for students who have been bullied. Schools and community groups also gathered for a Bully Walk on Saturday at BB&T Ballpark.

It’s important that students themselves are getting involved to combat bullying. They may be the solution because while bullies may seem like fearless rebels, most often they are troubled and insecure and looking for acceptance. The last thing they probably want is to be on the outside.

Which brings us to the parents of bullies, and you probably know who you are. Do your child a big favor and let them know that you also think that bullies are uncool.
