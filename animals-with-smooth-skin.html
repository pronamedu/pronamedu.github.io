Amphibians are Animals with wet and smooth skin.

About Amphibians

Amphibians are a group of vertebrates.

Animals belonging to the Amphibia class depend on the ambient temperature. This is because they cannot carry out metabolic processes to gain heat. Therefore, amphibians get energy by taking heat sources from the surrounding environment. This condition makes it a type of cold-blooded or poikilothermic animal.

Characteristics of Amphibians

Animals that are included in the Amphibia class have special characteristics that distinguish them from other animals. The following are the characteristics of amphibians:

Cold-blooded animals Have smooth to rough skin and lots of glands Skin tends to be slimy The heart consists of three chambers, one ventricle and two atria Reproduce by laying eggs and external fertilization Eggs covered in gelatin or mucus Experience a complete metamorphosis process Animals tetrapods or four-legged Respiratory organs differ when they are larvae and adults Eyes covered with membranes or nictitans membranes for diving

Types of amphibians

The amphibian group is further divided into three small groups, namely the orders Anura, caudata, and Gymnophiona. Here's the explanation:

Anura

The order Anura refers to frogs or frogs whose main characteristics are short stature, split tongue, bulging eyes, can jump, and no tail.

Caudata

Order caudata or salamanders which are not endemic to our country. There are salamander species that breathe using lungs, gills, or even skin.

Its body tends to be slimy to keep the skin moist when on land.

Gymnophiona

The order Gymnophiona consists of caecilian animals that resemble large worms or snakes. This group of animals is classified as rare because you can only find it in the depths of the forest and river or swamp areas.

This order of amphibians also has the ability to dig in loose to muddy soil thanks to its hard head.

21. animals that spend their life both in water and land . They have smooth ,moist and scale less skin​


Answer:

Amphibians are cold-blooded animals that possess backbones and display features that lie between those of fish and reptiles. They spend time both in water and on land. Their larvae (not yet fully developed offspring) mature in water and breathe through gills, like fish, while adults breathe air through lungs and skin. Amphibians are in the class Amphibia, which includes over 3,500 species. They are further divided into three orders: Anura (frogs and toads), Urodela (salamanders and newts), and Gymnophiona (caecilians, pronounced sih-SILL-yuhns, which are wormlike in appearance).

History

Amphibians evolved from fish about 400 million years ago, when the amount of dry land on Earth increased greatly. Certain fish adapted to these changing conditions by gradually developing limbs to crawl with and lungs to breathe with. Such organisms, capable of life both in water and on land, came to be called amphibians, a name that means "double life." Amphibians were the first vertebrates (animals with backbones) to live on land. However, they returned to the water to breed. The largest variety of amphibians occurred about 360 to 230 million years ago, when the environment was continually alternating between wet and dry conditions. Many of the species that developed during this period no longer exist. The groups of amphibians that survived to the present day can be traced back no further than 200 million years.
