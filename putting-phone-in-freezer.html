9. Put water in the plastic bag and sealed it, put it in the freezer. What happened?


Answer:

As the water cools it freezes and expands with a 9% volume increase. This increases the pressure within the container. As the pressure increases, the freezing point decreases. When the water reaches -18 C, the pressure at the freezing is about 180 MPa (thanks to the detailed water phase diagram posted by Mr Medrano). As the water cannot get any colder, there will be an equilibrium point between water and ice, much the same as a (much hotter) container that has steam and water, or any other liquid at equilibrium with its vapor. In the end, the water within the container will be partially frozen, at a pressure of 180 MPa (about 26,500 psi or 1800 atmospheres).

It will not fully freeze. If it did, the pressure would have to compress the ice by 9%. The phase diagram also shows density contour lines. Following the 1100 kg/m3 line — the density of the water compressed 10% — you can see that the freezing point is about -20 or -21 C, which is lower than the freezer temperature.

The ‘cool’ thing is, if you then release the pressure, the water will be supercooled and instantly freeze.
