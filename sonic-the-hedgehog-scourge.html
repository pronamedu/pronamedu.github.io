7. what is the difference of scourging pads and sponge?​


Answer:

The scouring pad has been reincarnated in several forms over the years. In 1928, R. B. Kingman patented the scouring ball, which was one of the first scouring pads made of a metal mesh.[1]

On 30 September 1941, United States patent 2,257,456 was issued to Joseph R. Crockford of Chicago, IL for a scouring device of the type employed for "cleansing and scouring pots, pans and other household implements." The invention provided for a "plate of non-corrosive metal, arched or curved to fit the fingers."

In 1942, David J. Kelman patented a toroidal metal scouring pad. This was, and still is, used as an abrasive scrubbing pad, however, it is becoming less common, as it removes non-stick surfaces off pans.[1]

In 1988, the first scouring pad made of out sponge material was patented by Hans J. Hartmann. This enabled the scouring pad to float. To achieve this, he used a technique to thread the metal mesh through the sponge and wrap it around the outside. This was meant to stop the scouring pad from dropping to the bottom of the sink.[1]

In 1973, the plastic needle surface (now more commonly used) was invented by Edward Mednick, who invented the new surface as a way of reducing the damage scouring pads did to non-stick surfaces. This technology and varieties thereon are what most scouring pads sold today are based upon.[1]

26. Define Sonic Energy, no plagiarism


Answer:

Sonic energy, also known as sound energy, refers to the energy that is transferred through vibrations of sound waves. This energy is generated by the movement of objects, such as the vibration of vocal cords or the striking of a drum. The speed and intensity of the sound waves determine the amount of energy that is transferred. This energy can travel through different mediums, such as air, water, or solids, and can cause physical changes in the environment, such as the movement of objects or the heating of materials. The study of sonic energy is important in many fields, including acoustics, music, and medicine, where it is used for diagnostic purposes or therapeutic applications.
