28. physics quantities and units Reflection?​


In physics, reflection refers to the phenomenon of waves (such as light, sound, or water waves) bouncing off a surface. When a wave encounters a surface that it cannot pass through (such as a solid wall), some or all of the wave energy is reflected back. The amount of reflection that occurs depends on the properties of the surface and the type of wave.

There are several quantities that are used to describe reflection in physics:

Reflectivity: This is a measure of how much of the incident wave is reflected by a surface. It is often expressed as a percentage, with 100% reflectivity indicating that all of the incident wave is reflected and 0% reflectivity indicating that none of it is reflected.

Reflectance: This is a measure of how much of the reflected wave is actually received by an observer. It is often expressed as a ratio, with a reflectance of 1 indicating that all of the reflected wave is received and a reflectance of 0 indicating that none of it is received.

Refraction: This is the bending of a wave as it passes through a medium with a different refractive index. When a wave encounters a surface that it can pass through (such as a lens or the atmosphere), it may change direction due to refraction.

Units for these quantities depend on the type of wave being considered. For example, reflectivity and reflectance are often expressed in terms of decibels (dB) for electromagnetic waves (such as light or radio waves), while they may be expressed in terms of pressure or displacement for sound waves. Refractive index is typically expressed as a dimensionless quantity.
