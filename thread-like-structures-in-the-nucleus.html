16. a structure found in the nucleus


Answer:

In cell biology, the nucleus (pl. nuclei; from Latin nucleus or nuculeus, meaning kernel or seed) is a membrane-bound organelle found in eukaryotic cells. Eukaryotes usually have a single nucleus, but a few cell types, such as mammalian red blood cells, have no nuclei, and a few others including osteoclasts have many. The main structures making up the nucleus are the nuclear envelope, a double membrane that encloses the entire organelle and isolates its contents from the cellular cytoplasm; and the nuclear matrix (which includes the nuclear lamina), a network within the nucleus that adds mechanical support, much like the cytoskeleton supports the cell as a whole.

21. A diseased cell is no longer able to produce proteins. Which cell structure is most likely malfunctioning? nucleus cytoplasm ribosome mitochondrion


Answer:

When the cell is no longer able to produce proteins, the ribosomes are not performing their functions.

Explanation:

Ribosomes are one of the non-membranous organelles of the cell. Ribosomes  are responsible for manufacturing proteins in the cytoplasm through the process of protein synthesis. Ribosomes use the information from the DNA of the cell through the messenger RNA (mRNA). One functional unit of ribosome include one small ribosomal subunit and one large ribosomal subunit. Prior to protein synthesis, the small and large ribosomal subunit fuse with an mRNA transcript in the cytoplasm. The two types of ribosomes are the free ribosomes and the fixed ribosomes. Free ribosomes are scattered throughout the cytoplasm and makes proteins which are released directly into the cytosol. Fixed ribosomes are attached to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and make proteins which proteins that enter the ER for further modification.

The nucleus contains the DNA of the cell. The information in the DNA is used to manufacture proteins in the cytoplasm. An mRNA transcript is made from the information obtained in the DNA.

The cytoplasm is the part of the cell which contains both the non-membranous organelles and the membranous organelles. The liquid part of the cytoplasm is called the cytosol. It serves as the site for several biochemical processes such as glycolysis and protein synthesis.

The mitochondrion is the powerhouse of the cell. It is the organelle which is responsible for the majority of the ATPs produced in the cell through oxidative phosphorylation.

25. The DNA threads which appear inside the nucleus at the time of cell division.​


The DNA threads which appear inside the nucleus at the time of cell division are called chromatin.

Detail of explanation:

Cell division is a process in which a cell divides into two or more parts to form a new cell. There are three types of cell division: Amitosis, Mitosis and Meiosis.

Amitosis

Amitosis is a direct division. Amitosis generally occurs in prokaryotic organisms (organisms without a cell nucleus). For example, in bacteria. Amitotic division is also present in eukaryotic organisms such as protozoa. The process of amitosis begins with the attachment of chromosomes to the cell wall, then the cell will enlarge due to DNA replication. After the new DNA is formed, the cell reaches its maximum size and will divide into two parts with the same genetic material and size.

Mitosis

Mitosis occurs in somatic cells or living cells. Mitosis is the division of cells in succession by maintaining the same chromosomes so as to produce 2 daughter cells with genetically identical characters to the parent cell.The cell cycle consists of the stages of Interphase, Karyokinesis and Cytokinesis.

The interphase or resting phase, consists of 3 stages, namely the growth phase (G1), the synthesis or multiplication phase (S), and the last phase is preparation for division (G2).

Karyokinesis or cell division consists of Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase and Telophase.

Prophase: at this stage, the centrosome divides into 2 centrioles which will move in opposite directions. The nuclear membrane begins to fade because the core will divide, inside which there are fine threads called chromatin. In the next stage, the centrioles start moving in the opposite direction. Chromatin thickens into chromosomes.Prometaphase: the late prophase and early metaphase stages of the nuclear membrane begin to fade and chromosomes begin to form, the chromosomes begin to move in the equatorial plane, the centrioles are already at their respective poles in opposite directions.Metaphase: Chromosomes are already lined up at the equator, each chromosome is tied by centriole threadsAnaphase: Chromosomes separate towards opposite polesTelophase begins to form 2 cells, later the centrioles become centrosomes, and the cell membrane begins to form and the cell nucleus begins to form again.

This cycle is closed with the stage of Cytokinesis or the division of the cytoplasm. The cycle continues to repeat with the same phases and stages.

Meiosis

Meiotic cell division occurs only in gonosomal cells (sex cells). Meiosis is a process of cell division that produces daughter cells with half the number of chromosomes from the parent cell.

Meiosis itself occurs through two processes, namely meiosis I and meiosis II. In the meiosis I process, there will usually be a reduction process on the chromosomes consisting of phases of Prophase 1, Metaphase 1, Anaphase 1, and Telophase 1. In the meiosis II process, mitotic cell division actually consists of phases of Prophase 2, Metaphase 2, Anaphase 2 , and Telophase 2.
