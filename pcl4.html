To answer this, calculate the reaction quotient, Q. Q has the same form as Keq, but the concentrations are not necessarily equilibrium ones. So, for this question: 

Q = [PCl5]/[PCl3][Cl2] = 0.6/(0.1)(0.15) = 40 

Because Q > Kc, the reaction must move to the left to reach equilibrium. This will make less PCl5 so that the reaction will proceed in the direction of forming more PCl3 and Cl2 until equilibrium is reached.

Answer:

We can use Hess's law to calculate ∆H for the reaction PCl5 (g) → PCl3 (g) + Cl2 (g) using the given data. Hess's law states that the total enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the pathway taken, as long as the initial and final conditions are the same.

First, we need to reverse the second equation and multiply it by 6 to get the same number of moles of Cl2 as in the first equation:

6P4 (s) + 30Cl2 (g) → 24PCl5 (g) ∆H = 6(-3438 kJ) = -20628 kJ

Next, we need to cancel out the P4 (s) in the first equation with the P4 (s) in the second equation. We can do this by multiplying the first equation by 4:

4P4 (s) + 24Cl2 (g) → 16PCl3 (g) ∆H = 4(-2439 kJ) = -9756 kJ

Finally, we can add the two equations together, canceling out the 16PCl3 (g) on both sides:

6P4 (s) + 30Cl2 (g) → 24PCl5 (g) ∆H = -20628 kJ

4P4 (s) + 24Cl2 (g) → 16PCl3 (g) ∆H = -9756 kJ

6P4 (s) + 54Cl2 (g) → 24PCl5 (g) + 16Cl2 (g) ∆H = -30384 kJ

The overall reaction is the same as the reaction we want to find ∆H for, except that it includes an extra 16 moles of Cl2 (g) on the product side. To get the ∆H for the desired reaction, we can subtract the ∆H for the extra 16 moles of Cl2 (g):

∆H = -30384 kJ - (16 mol x 0 kJ/mol) = -30384 kJ

Therefore, the ∆H for the reaction PCl5 (g) → PCl3 (g) + Cl2 (g) is -30384 kJ.

21. Kc = 0.040 for the system below at 450oC. If a reaction is initiated with 0.40 mole of Cl2 and 0.40 mole of PCl3 in a 2.0 liter container, what is the equilibrium concentration of Cl2 in the same system?PCl5(g)  PCl3(g) + Cl2(g)(a) 0.07 M(b) 0.16 M(c) 0.11 M(d) 0.04 M(e) 0.26 M​


Given:

[tex]K_{\text{c}} = 0.040[/tex]

[tex][\text{PCl}_{3}]_{0} = \frac{\text{0.40 mol}}{\text{2.0 L}} = \text{0.20 M}[/tex]

[tex][\text{Cl}_{2}]_{0} = \frac{\text{0.40 mol}}{\text{2.0 L}} = \text{0.20 M}[/tex]

balanced chemical equation: PCl₅ (g) [tex]\rightleftharpoons[/tex] PCl₃ (g) + Cl₂ (g)

Required:

[tex][\text{Cl}_{2}][/tex]

Solution:

Step 1: Construct the ICE table.

[tex]\begin{array}{cccccccc} & \text{PCl}_{5}(g) & \rightleftharpoons & \text{PCl}_{3}(g) & + & \text{Cl}_{2}(aq) \\ \text{Initial} \: (M): & 0 & & 0.20 & & 0.20 \\ \text{Change} \: (M): & + x & & - x & & - x \\ \hline \text{Equilibrium} \: (M): & + x & & 0.20 - x & & 0.20 - x \\ \end{array}[/tex]

Step 2: Write the equilibrium constant expression.

[tex]K_{\text{c}} = \frac{[\text{PCl}_{3}][\text{Cl}_{2}]}{[\text{PCl}_{5}]}[/tex]

Step 3: Solve for the value of x.

[tex]0.040 = \frac{(0.20 - x)(0.20 - x)}{x}[/tex]

[tex]0.040 = \frac{0.040 - 0.40x + x^{2}}{x}[/tex]

[tex]0.040x = 0.040 - 0.40x + x^{2}[/tex]

[tex]x^{2} - 0.44x + 0.040 = 0[/tex]

By using quadratic formula

a = 1 , b = -0.44 , c = 0.040

[tex]x = \frac{-b \: ± \: \sqrt{b^{2} - 4ac}}{2a}[/tex]

[tex]x = \frac{-(-0.44) \: ± \: \sqrt{(-0.44)^{2} - 4(1)(0.040)}}{2(1)}[/tex]

[tex]x = 0.31 \: M \: (reject)[/tex]

[tex]x = 0.13 \: M \: (accept)[/tex]

We reject 0.31 M because it is greater than the initial concentration of Cl₂. There is no such thing as negative concentration. Therefore, we will use 0.13 M as the value of x.

Step 4: Calculate the equilibrium concentration of Cl₂.

[tex][\text{Cl}_{2}] = 0.20 - x[/tex]

[tex][\text{Cl}_{2}] = 0.20 - 0.13[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{[\text{Cl}_{2}] = \text{0.07 M}}[/tex]

Answer:

(a) 0.07 M
