9. RP1 ACT2B COMPARING AND CONTRASTING EXPRESSIONS. Without going back to the lessons in this module, compare and contrast each given pair of expression. Words in Pairs Comparison Contrast 1. Research vs. Report Writing 2. Author-date system Humanities VS. system 3. MLA vs. APA 4. One-line quotation vs. Five- line quotation 5. Conclusion vs. Recommendation Left-Right 6. margin Top-Bottom margin 7. Abstract vs. Introduction 8. language Literary Language VS. Academic VS. 9. Passive voice vs. Active voice 10. Research report VS. Literature report​


Answer:

1.) Research vs. Report Writing

Comparison: Both research and report writing involve gathering and presenting information.

Contrast: Research is the process of investigating a topic or problem, while report writing is the act of presenting the findings and conclusions of the research.

2.) Author-date system vs. Humanities VS. system

Comparison: Both systems are used to cite sources in academic writing.

Contrast: The Author-date system is commonly used in the social sciences and sciences, while the Humanities VS. system is commonly used in the humanities.

3.) MLA vs. APA

Comparison: Both are citation styles used in academic writing.

Contrast: MLA is primarily used in the humanities, while APA is primarily used in the social sciences.

4.) One-line quotation vs. Five-line quotation

Comparison: Both types of quotations are used to incorporate the ideas of others into one's own writing.

Contrast: One-line quotations are shorter and are generally used to support smaller points, while five-line quotations are longer and are generally used to support larger points.

5.) Conclusion vs. Recommendation

Comparison: Both conclusion and recommendation provide an end to the discussion and offer a course of action.

Contrast: Conclusions are drawn from the results of a study, while recommendations are suggestions for future actions.

6.) Margin Top-Bottom margin

Comparison: Both margins serve a similar purpose, to provide space between the text and the edge of the page.

Contrast: The top and bottom margins determine the distance between the text and the page edges at the top and bottom of the page, respectively.

7.) Abstract vs. Introduction

Comparison: Both abstracts and introductions provide an overview of the paper.

Contrast: Abstracts are brief summaries of the paper's main points, while introductions provide background information, context, and thesis statements.

8.) Literary Language VS. Academic Language

Comparison: Both types of language are used in writing.

Contrast: Literary language is used in literature and creative writing, while academic language is used in scholarly research and writing.

9.) Passive voice vs. Active voice

Comparison: Both passive and active voice are grammatical constructions used to convey meaning in writing.

Contrast: Passive voice emphasizes the object or recipient of the action, while active voice emphasizes the subject or doer of the action.

10.) Research report VS. Literature report

Comparison: Both types of reports present information on a topic.

Contrast: Research reports present findings from research studies, while literature reports analyze existing literature on a topic.

1. Focus vs. Epicenter

Difference:

The main difference between focus and epicentre is their location. The focus is that part of the earth where the rocks that are stressed under the surface of the earth is present. On the other hand, the part of the earth’s surface that is present right below the focus is known as the epicentre.

Similarities:

Both determinants of the origin of the ground movements.

2. Active volcano vs. Dormant volcano

Difference:

Active volcanoes have a recent history of eruptions; they are likely to erupt again. Dormant volcanoes have not erupted for a very long time but may erupt at a future time.

Similarities:

Dormant volcano is similar to an active volcano since it has the probability or a likelihood to erupt like an active volcano.

3. Compression vs. Tension vs. Shearing

Difference:

Tension or stress is the opposing force produced by a body in reaction of imposing a force on it e.g. rope chain, beams, or any body under mechanical stress.

Compression is a force applied to a body longitudinally inwards so that the length of the body reduces by dl and thus increase in other two dimension by infinitesimally smaller value.

Shear is an unaligned force exerted on a body where upper half is in one direction and other half is in opposite direction to the shear plane. The shear tension is produced only at the plane of shear formed.

4. Tectonic earthquake vs. Volcanic earthquake

Difference:

Tectonic earthquakes are caused by the movement of plates when energy accumulated within plate boundary zones is released.

Tectonic earthquakes are usually larger than volcanic earthquakes.

Similarities:

Both volcanoes and earthquakes occur due to movement of the Earth's tectonic plates.

5. Effusive eruption vs. Explosive eruption

Difference:

When gas and broken fragments of magma are shot up into the atmosphere, this is called an explosive eruption. The broken fragments are called pyroclasts, or if they are smaller than 2 mm we call it volcanic ash.

When lava flows out of a volcano, this is called an effusive eruption.

23. Give the difference and similarities of the 6 principles of page Designing. 1. Harmony vs Emphasis ______________________________________________________________ 2. Balance vs Proportion ______________________________________________________________ 3 Movement vs Contrasts ______________________________________________________________


Answer:

Balance

Visual balance comes from arranging the elements so that no section is heavier than another. Sometimes elements are thrown out of balance to give emphasis or create a certain mood.

Proximity / Unity

The relationship between people or elements in a design is affected by proximity. How close or far apart elements are, suggest a relationship or lack of a relationship with each other. Unity can be created by using a third element to connect distant parts. Are all the title elements together? Is contact information altogether? Are all related elements together? In design proximity or closeness creates a bond or a link.

Alignment

Alignment brings order to the design. How type and graphics are aligned on a page and in relation to each other makes the layout easier or more difficult to read. Has a grid been used? Is there a common alignment? RHS, LHS, centred? Does the alignment aid or hinder readability? Has it been done with a specific goal in mind?

Repetition / Consistency

Repeating design elements and consistency of style shows a reader where to go and helps them navigate the design. Do the page numbers appear in the same place from page to page? Are headlines consistent in size style, placement? Is the style consistent throughout?

Contrast

In design, big and small elements black and white text and graphics all create contrast. Contrast helps different elements stand out. Does the design have enough contrast? Is there enough contrast between text and background for it to stand out and be readable? Are the more important elements, headlines, calls to action contrasting enough to stand out?

White space

Designs that cram too much into a small space are uncomfortable and may be confusing and difficult to read. White space gives the design breathing space. Is there enough space between columns of text? Does text sit clear of the graphics? But beware too much white space and the elements will seem to float on the page.

Sure, here are the meanings of the 5 elements of plot and the 6 types of story conflict:

5 Elements of Plot:

1. Exposition - This is the introduction of the story where the characters, setting, and initial conflict are introduced. It sets the stage for the story and provides important background information.

2. Rising Action - This is the part of the story where the conflict starts to develop and the tension begins to rise. It includes a series of events that lead up to the climax.

3. Climax - This is the turning point of the story and the most intense part. It is the moment of greatest tension, where the main character faces the biggest challenge.

4. Falling Action - This is the part of the story where the tension starts to decrease, and the events begin to wind down. It leads to the resolution of the conflict.

5. Conclusion - This is the final part of the story where the loose ends are tied up, and the story comes to a close. It provides closure to the story and gives the reader a sense of satisfaction.

6 Types of Story Conflict:

1. Character vs. Supernatural - This is a conflict where the character is pitted against supernatural forces like ghosts, monsters, or other paranormal entities.

2. Character vs. Technology - This is a conflict where the character faces challenges with technology or machines. It could be a malfunctioning computer system, a deadly robot, or some other technological issue.

3. Character vs. Society - This is a conflict where the character is pitted against the norms, beliefs, or laws of society. It could be an individual struggling to fit into society or challenging its rules and expectations.

4. Character vs. Nature - This is a conflict where the character faces challenges from the natural world. It could be a natural disaster, a wild animal, or extreme weather conditions.

5. Character vs. Self - This is a conflict where the character struggles with their own beliefs, emotions, or desires. It could be a battle with addiction, a crisis of conscience, or an internal struggle to overcome fears or insecurities.

6. Character vs. Character - This is a conflict where the character is pitted against another character. It could be a physical fight or a battle of wits between two characters with opposing goals or values.
