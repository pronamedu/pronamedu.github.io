1. Simile and personification in the poem how do i love thee


“I love thee freely, as men strive for right” (simile) – Using the word “as” to establish the simile, the speaker compares the way in which she loves her husband to the way in which mankind strives to do good in the world. Her love is as freely and spontaneously given as the dedication of men who strive to accomplish good things for humanity.

“I love thee purely, as they turn from praise” (simile) – Using the word “as” to establish the simile, the speaker compares the pure and humble nature of her love to the humility that decent men exhibit. Just as decent human beings commit good deeds without expecting praise in return, she loves purely without expectation of reward,.

“By sun and candle-light” (metaphor) – The mention of sun and candle-light serves as a metaphor for the passage of time and the course of one’s life. The speaker’s love fills her days and keeps her going through life.

“I love thee to the depth and breadth and height/ My soul can reach” (metaphor) – The speaker attempts to quantify her love by measuring the physical space it takes up. As love is an abstract concept which cannot be measured, the references to these measurements are intended metaphorically to convey the immensity of her love.

16. Give a poem that has hyperbole and personification​


The wind howled like a pack of wolves,

As it tore through the streets with its powerful gales.

It snatched hats and umbrellas with ease,

And knocked over trash cans and mail.

The rain fell like a torrential flood,

Drowning the city in its endless tears.

It turned streets into rivers of mud,

And sent people running for cover in fear.

The lightning struck like a fiery sword,

Splitting the sky with its brilliant light.

It lit up the darkness like a Lord,

And sent thunder crashing through the night.

But even in the midst of the storm,

The city stood tall and unbroken.

For even the wind, rain and lightning's swarm,

Could not bring it down, forever spoken.

This poem uses hyperbole in describing the wind as howling like a pack of wolves, the rain falling like a torrential flood, and the lightning striking like a fiery sword. These exaggerations are used to emphasize the intense and powerful nature of the storm. The poem also uses personification by giving human-like characteristics to the wind (tearing through the streets and snatching hats), the rain (drowning the city in its endless tears), and the lightning (splitting the sky with its brilliant light). These literary devices create a vivid and dramatic imagery that gives the reader a sense of the power and force of the storm.

"Nature's Fury"

The storm raged like a beast,

With all its might and might.

Howling through the night,

Tearing down the houses,

Rising high and then crashing down,

Drowning ships and sailors,

Without a single frown.

The rain was like a river,

Flowing with a force so strong,

Drowning all in its path,

For far too long.

The fire was like a dragon,

Breathing out a deadly breath,

Leaving nothing but ash,

In its fiery death.

Hyperbole and personification are used to exaggerate and imbue non-human things with human characteristics. In this poem, the storm, winds, waves, rain, and fire are described in an exaggerated or personified manner, giving them a sense of power and agency.
