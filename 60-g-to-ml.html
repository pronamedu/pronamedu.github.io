7. What is the molal concentration of a solution containing 1.5 g CH3COOH (MM= 60 g/mol) and 1000 g H₂0 (density=1.00 g/mL)?​


Answer: The molal concentration of the given solution is 0.025 mol/kg.

Explanation: To find the molal concentration of the given solution, we first need to calculate the number of moles of CH3COOH present in the solution. The molar mass of CH3COOH is 60 g/mol. Therefore, the number of moles of CH3COOH current in 1.5 g of CH3COOH is 1.5 g / 60 g/mol = 0.025 moles. Next, we need to calculate the mass of the solvent (H2O) in the solution. The mass of H2O in the solution is 1000 g, and the density of H2O is 1.00 g/mL. Therefore, the volume of H2O present in the solution is 1000 mL / 1.00 g/mL = 1000 mL = 1 L. The mass of 1 L of H2O is 1000 g. Thus, the molality of the solution can be calculated using the formula: molality = moles of solute/mass of solvent in kg. Substituting the values, we get molality = 0.025 moles / 1 kg = 0.025 mol/kg.

9. Leonora prepared a sugar solution by dissolving 60 g of sugar to 700 mL of water. What is the percent by mass of sugar?​


Given:

mass of solute = 60 g

volume of solvent = 700 mL

Required:

percent by mass

Formula:

[tex]\text{percent by mass} = \dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solute + mass of solvent}} \times 100[/tex]

Solution:

Solving for the mass of solvent (water)

Note: The density of water is 1 g/mL.

mass of solvent = density of solvent × volume of solvent

mass of solvent = 1 g/mL × 700 mL

mass of solvent = 700 g

Solving for the percent by mass

[tex]\text{percent by mass} = \dfrac{\text{60 g}}{\text{60 g + 700 g}} \times 100[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{percent by mass} = 7.9\%}[/tex]

Final Answer:

percent by mass = 7.9%

14. What is the molar concentration of a solution containing 1.5 g CH₂COOH (MM= 60 g/mol) and 1000 g H₂0 (density=1.00 g/mL)?​


First, we need to calculate the number of moles of CH₂COOH:

Number of moles = mass / molecular weight

Number of moles = 1.5 g / 60 g/mol

Number of moles = 0.025 mol

Next, we need to calculate the volume of the solution in liters:

Volume = mass / density

Volume = 1000 g / 1.00 g/mL

Volume = 1000 mL = 1 L

Now we can calculate the molar concentration of the solution:

Molar concentration = number of moles / volume

Molar concentration = 0.025 mol / 1 L

Molar concentration = 0.025 M

Therefore, the molar concentration of the solution is 0.025 M.

Answer:

0.025 mol/L.

Explanation:

To calculate the molar concentration of a solution, we need to first determine the number of moles of solute (CH₂COOH) and the volume of the solution (H₂O) in liters.

Given:

Mass of CH₂COOH (solute) = 1.5 g

Molar mass of CH₂COOH = 60 g/mol

Mass of H₂O (solvent) = 1000 g

Density of H₂O = 1.00 g/mL

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of CH₂COOH

Number of moles of CH₂COOH = Mass of CH₂COOH / Molar mass of CH₂COOH

Number of moles of CH₂COOH = 1.5 g / 60 g/mol

Number of moles of CH₂COOH = 0.025 mol

Step 2: Calculate the volume of the solution (H₂O) in liters

Volume of H₂O = Mass of H₂O / Density of H₂O

Volume of H₂O = 1000 g / 1.00 g/mL

Volume of H₂O = 1000 mL = 1 L

Step 3: Calculate the molar concentration of the solution

Molar concentration (molarity) = Number of moles of solute / Volume of solution (in liters)

Molar concentration (molarity) = 0.025 mol / 1 L

Molar concentration (molarity) = 0.025 mol/L

Therefore, the molar concentration of the solution containing 1.5 g of CH₂COOH and 1000 g of H₂O is 0.025 mol/L.

22. 1. A 60-ml solution contains 8.6 g of ethanol and 0.042 g of antiseptic. Calculate the concentration of each component in mass/volume percent. Ans. Ethanol = 14%; antiseptic = 0.70%​


✏️PERCENT BY MASS PER VOLUME

[tex]\sf{\large{\red{SOLUTION:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & \text{volume of solution} = \text{60 mL} \\ & \text{mass of ethanol} = \text{8.6 g} \\ & \text{mass of antiseptic} = \text{0.042 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Final Step: Calculate the concentration in mass/volume percent of each component.

• For ethanol

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{percent by mass per volume} & = \frac{\text{mass of ethanol}}{\text{volume of solution}} \times \frac{\text{1 mL}}{\text{1 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \frac{\text{8.6 g}}{\text{60 mL}} \times \frac{\text{1 mL}}{\text{1 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \boxed{14\%} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For antiseptic

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{percent by mass per volume} & = \frac{\text{mass of antiseptic}}{\text{volume of solution}} \times \frac{\text{1 mL}}{\text{1 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \frac{\text{0.042 g}}{\text{60 mL}} \times \frac{\text{1 mL}}{\text{1 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \boxed{0.070\%} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the concentrations of ethanol and antiseptic in percent by mass per volume are 14% and 0.070%, respectively.

What is molarity?

In terms of the amount of material per unit volume of solution, molar concentration is a measurement of the concentration of a chemical species, specifically a solute in a solution. The most frequent measure of molarity in chemistry is the number of moles per liter, denoted by the unit symbol mol/L or mol/dm³ in SI units.

The total number of moles of solute in one liter of a particular solution is known as its molarity. Since, unlike mass, the volume of the system fluctuates with changes in the system's physical parameters, the molality of a solution is dependent on changes in the system's physical qualities like pressure and temperature. M, which is referred to as a molar, stands for molarity. One molar refers to a solution's molarity, which is defined as one gram of solute dissolved in one litre of solution. The complete volume of the solution is taken because, as we know, a solution is created when a solvent and a solute combine.

How to calculate molarity?

By dividing the total number of moles of solute by the total volume of liters of solution, we may get a solution's molarity.

Formula:

M = n/v

M = molar concentration

n = moles of solute

v = liters of solution

[tex]\begin{aligned} & mass_{\text{solute}} = \text{30 g} \\ & MM_{\text{solute}} = \text{60 g/mol} \\ & V_{\text{solution}} = \text{250 mL = 0.25 L} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = \frac{mass_{\text{solute}}}{MM_{\text{solute}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{30 g}}{\text{60 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{0.5 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molarity of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} M & = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{V_{\text{solution}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.5 mol}}{\text{0.25 L}} \\ & = \text{2 mol/L} \\ & = \boxed{2 \: M} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the resulting molarity of the pickling solution is 2 M.
