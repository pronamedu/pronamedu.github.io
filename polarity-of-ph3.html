19. A sample of phosphine (PH3) has a volume of 11.2 L. How many moles of PH3 are in this sample?​


Answer:

To determine the number of moles of PH3 in a sample, you would use the ideal gas law equation, PV = nRT.

Where:

P = pressure

V = volume

n = number of moles

R = ideal gas constant

T = temperature

Since the pressure and temperature are not given, we can assume that the sample is at standard conditions (1 atm and 273 K). We can then use the ideal gas constant, R, which is 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K.

We can then rearrange the ideal gas law equation to solve for n:

n = (PV)/(RT)

If we insert the given values, we get:

n = (1 x 11.2 L) / (0.0821 L·atm/mol·K x 273 K)

We get that:

n = 0.16 mol

Therefore, the sample of phosphine (PH3) has a volume of 11.2 L contains 0.16 mol of PH3.

22. How do I balance P4(s) → H2PO2- (aq) + PH3(g) in an acidic solution?


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: Separate the equation into two half-reactions.

Note that the oxidation numbers of P in P₄(s) and H₂PO₂⁻(aq) are 0 and +1, respectively. (oxidation)

Note that the oxidation numbers of P in P₄(s) and H₂PO₂⁻(aq) are 0 and +1, respectively. (oxidation)Note that the oxidation numbers of P in P₄(s) and PH₃(g) are 0 and -3, respectively. (reduction)

Oxidation: ½P₄(s) → H₂PO₂⁻(aq)

Reduction: ½P₄(s) → PH₃(g)

Step 2: Balance each half-reaction for number and type of atoms and charges. For reactions in acidic medium, add H₂O to balance the O atoms and H⁺ to balance the H atoms.

Oxidation half-reaction: The P atoms are not already balanced. To balance them, we need two moles of H₂PO₂⁻(aq):

½P₄(s) → 2H₂PO₂⁻(aq)

Because the reaction takes place in an acidic medium, we add four H₂O molecules to the left-hand side of the arrow to balance the O atoms:

½P₄(s) + 4H₂O (l) → 2H₂PO₂⁻(aq)

To balance the H atoms, we add four H⁺ ions on the right side:

½P₄(s) + 4H₂O (l) → 2H₂PO₂⁻(aq) + 4H⁺(aq)

There are now no charge on the left side and two positive charges on the right side. Therefore, we add two electrons on the right:

½P₄(s) + 4H₂O (l) → 2H₂PO₂⁻(aq) + 4H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻

Reduction half-reaction: The P atoms are not already balanced. To balance them, we need two moles of PH₃(g):

½P₄(s) → 2PH₃(g)

To balance the H atoms, we add six H⁺ ions on the left side:

½P₄(s) + 6H⁺(aq) → 2PH₃(g)

There are now six positive charges on the left side and no charge on the right side. Therefore, we add six electrons on the left:

½P₄(s) + 6H⁺(aq) + 6e⁻ → 2PH₃(g)

Step 3: Add the two half-equations together and balance the final equation by inspection. The electrons on both sides must cancel. If the oxidation and reduction half-reactions contain different numbers of electrons, we need to multiply one or both half-reactions to equalize the number of electrons.

Since both half-reactions contain different numbers of electrons, we multiply the oxidation half-reaction by 3 and the reduction half-reaction by 1

Oxidation: (3/2)P₄(s) + 12H₂O(l) → 6H₂PO₂⁻(aq) + 12H⁺(aq) + 6e⁻

Reduction: ½P₄(s) + 6H⁺(aq) + 6e⁻ → 2PH₃(g)

Adding the two half-reactions, we obtain

[tex]\begin{array}{ccccccccc} & & \frac{3}{2}\text{P}_4(s) & + & 12\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) & \rightarrow & 6\text{H}_2\text{PO}_2^-(aq) & + & 12\text{H}^+(aq) & + & 6e^- \\ \frac{1}{2}\text{P}_4(s) & + & 6\text{H}^+(aq) & + & 6e^- & \rightarrow & 2\text{PH}_3(g) \\ \hline & & 2\text{P}_4(s) & + & 12\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) & \rightarrow & 6\text{H}_2\text{PO}_2^-(aq) & + & 2\text{PH}_3(g) & + & 6\text{H}^+(aq) \end{array}[/tex]

Reducing the coefficients in lowest whole numbers, the balanced redox equation is

[tex]\boxed{\text{P}_4(s) + 6\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow 3\text{H}_2\text{PO}_2^-(aq) + \text{PH}_3(g) + 3\text{H}^+(aq)}[/tex]

Explanation:

NH3 is polar because it has 3 dipoles that do not cancel out. Each N-H bond is polar because N is more electronegative than H. NH3 is overall asymmetrical in its VSEPR shape, so the dipoles don't cancel out and it is therefore polar
