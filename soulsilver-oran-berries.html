Answer:

In the novel "The Plague" by Albert Camus, the first sign that things are going wrong in Oran is the sudden appearance of dead rats in the streets. The rats, initially thought to have been poisoned, begin to appear in large numbers and are seen dead in the gutters, on the stairs, and in other public areas.

At first, the citizens of Oran dismissed the rats as a nuisance, and the authorities began organizing a cleanup effort to dispose of the dead animals. However, as the days pass, the number of dead rats continues to increase, and it becomes clear that something more sinister is happening.

The citizens of Oran start to notice a strange odor in the air, and the death toll among rats becomes alarming. The people begin to feel uneasy and anxious about the situation, but they are uncertain about what is happening and how it will affect them.

Eventually, the people of Oran begin to fall ill with a mysterious and deadly disease that spreads rapidly throughout the city. It is only then that they realize the significance of the dead rats and the severity of the situation they are facing.

Thus, the appearance of dead rats is the first sign that things are going wrong in Oran and that the city is in the grip of a deadly epidemic.
