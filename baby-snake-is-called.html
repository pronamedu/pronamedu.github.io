1. Wy does baby snakes dangerous than adult snakes​


Answer:

Contrary to popular belief, baby snakes are not necessarily more dangerous than adult snakes. In fact, many species of snakes are more dangerous when they are fully grown and have developed larger venom glands and more potent venom.

Contrary to popular belief, baby snakes are not necessarily more dangerous than adult snakes. In fact, many species of snakes are more dangerous when they are fully grown and have developed larger venom glands and more potent venom. However, there are a few reasons why some people might perceive baby snakes as more dangerous:

1. Lack of control: Baby snakes may not yet have full control over their venom glands and may release venom more readily and in larger quantities than adult snakes. This can make them seem more dangerous, particularly if they are disturbed or feel threatened.

2. Size and visibility: Baby snakes are often smaller and less visible than adult snakes, which can make them more difficult to spot and avoid. This can increase the likelihood of accidental encounters and bites.

3. Inexperience: People who are not familiar with snakes may not know how to recognize and avoid potentially dangerous species, and may therefore assume that all snakes are equally dangerous, regardless of their age or size.

Overall, it's important to remember that snakes are generally not aggressive and will only bite if they feel threatened or provoked. If you encounter a snake, it's best to keep a safe distance and give it space to move away. If you are unsure whether a snake is venomous or not, it's always best to err on the side of caution and avoid handling it.

3. Baby snake explain growth and development


Answer:

Sure! Baby snakes, like all reptiles, go through a process called metamorphosis, which is the process of growing and developing from an egg into a juvenile and then into an adult.

During the egg stage, baby snakes are developing inside an eggshell and receive nutrients from the yolk. When the snake is ready to hatch, it will use a special tooth called an "egg tooth" to break through the eggshell and emerge.

After hatching, baby snakes are called hatchlings. They are usually very small, only a few inches long, and may not look very different from adult snakes. However, they are not yet fully developed and will continue to grow and mature.

Snakes grow by shedding their skin and growing a new, larger one. This process is called "molting." Snakes will go through several molts as they grow, and the rate at which they grow will depend on the species and the conditions in which they are living.

As snakes grow and mature, they will also go through various physical and behavioral changes. For example, they may change color, develop markings or patterns, and learn to hunt and defend themselves.
