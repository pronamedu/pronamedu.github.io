Primitive dance is a dance that evolved during the primitive society before civilization.The primitive period is the prehistoric era, namely the period before the emergence of the kingdom, so that it did not have a formal leader. Primitive dance moves are very simple. This primitive dance imitates animal movements and natural movements with hand movements, foot movements, and head movements while moving in a circle around the bonfire.

26. what are the contribution of primitive era in the development of philippine folk dance?​


The Contribution of The Primitive Era In The Philippine Folk Dance

The primitive era's contribution to Philippine folk dance is that it gave them a sense of identity. It also provided them with a sense of belonging. The Kumintang was the oldest Christianized Filipino group, with the oldest dance and song.

Further explanation:Origins

The majority of the folk dances from the mountainous region of the Philippines came from tribes' rituals, prayers, and celebrations. Folk dances in the Tagalog region of the Philippines (including the country's capital Manila) were heavily influenced by Spanish and European influences. Dances in the Visayan region of the country were primarily inspired by animal movements. As the only Philippine region never subjected to Spanish rule, folk dances in Mindanao, located in the southern Philippines, were heavily influenced by Muslim culture.

Ritual and Tribal Dances

Dancing was an important part of the lives of the cultural minorities who lived in the Philippines' mountain regions. Their tribal and ritual dances existed prior to the arrival of Christianity and Islam in the country. These Filipinos preserved their ancient dances, which were performed primarily for the gods, goddesses, and nature. These dances were inextricably linked to ceremonies, rituals, and sacrifices.

Influence of the Spanish

The more than 300-year Spanish occupation of the Philippines had a significant impact on Philippine folk dancing. When the Spanish introduced Christianity to the country, the religion reflected the majority of the stories and performances of Filipino folk dances. Apart from wearing Spanish-style clothing that had been slightly modified with Filipino elements, these dances also reflected the Filipinos' daily struggles under Spanish rule. Local resources, such as bamboo castanets and abanico, were also used as props by Filipino dancers (Asian fans).

The National Dance

Tinikling is the national dance of the Philippines. It gets its name from the word tikling, which refers to a native bird that roams around fields, crushes tree branches, and avoids farmer-set traps. The dance originated in the Visayan province of Leyte. It mimics the bird's distinct movements, speed, and grace by deftly navigating between fast-moving bamboo poles. Tinikling is performed by two people who beat, tap, and slide bamboo poles on the ground and against each other, while one or more dancers step over and in between the poles without getting their feet caught between them.

Dances of Fight and Celebration

Most Filipino folk dances, regardless of region, tell stories about fights and struggles, as well as celebrations and feasts. The maglalatik dance of Binan, Laguna, for example, is a mock-war dance depicting a battle between the Moros (Muslim Filipinos from Mindanao) and the Christians. The arnis, a native weapon made of two sticks that serve as extensions of the hands, is used in the sakuting dance of abra to depict a fight between Christian Filipinos and non-Christian natives of the Cordillera region.
