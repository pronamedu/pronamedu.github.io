Calculate the molar mass of water.

16. 100 g of CuSO4·5H2O to ions


Answer:

CuSO4·5H2O is copper (II) sulfate pentahydrate. When 100 g of CuSO4·5H2O is dissolved in water, it will dissociate into its constituent ions as follows:

CuSO4·5H2O → Cu2+ + SO4^2- + 5H2O

This means that one mole of copper (II) sulfate pentahydrate will yield one mole of copper ions (Cu2+), one mole of sulfate ions (SO4^2-), and five moles of water molecules (5H2O). To determine the number of moles of copper (II) sulfate pentahydrate in 100 g, we need to know its molar mass. The molar mass of CuSO4·5H2O is:

(1 x 63.55 g/mol for Cu) + (1 x 32.06 g/mol for S) + (4 x 16.00 g/mol for O) + (5 x 18.02 g/mol for H2O) = 249.68 g/mol

Therefore, 100 g of CuSO4·5H2O is equal to 100 g / 249.68 g/mol = 0.4002 moles. This means that when 0.4002 moles of CuSO4·5H2O is dissolved in water, it will yield 0.4002 moles of Cu2+ ions, 0.4002 moles of SO4^2- ions, and 2.001 moles of water molecules.

How do I find molar mass?

The molar mass is the mass of a given chemical element or chemical compound (g) divided by the amount of substance (mol). The molar mass of a compound can be calculated by adding the standard atomic masses (in g/mol) of the constituent atoms.

However, molar mass can also be calculated by multiplying the atomic mass in amu by the molar mass constant (1 g/mol). To calculate the molar mass of a compound with multiple atoms, sum all the atomic mass of the constituent atoms.
