FEUDALISM IN THE PHILIPPINES COMPARED TO FEUDALISM IN THE MIDDLE AGES

In medieval Europe, feudalism was a system of political and armed practices that peaked between the 9th and 15th centuries. It can be broadly characterized as a system for arranging relationships in society around the possession of land, often known as a fief, in return for labor or service.

In the Philippines, feudalism is predominantly agrarian in nature, with most farmers and peasants compelled to labor for a landlord who benefits from high land rents, exorbitant loan interest rates, and extremely low crop prices, among other things.

Many farmers in the Philippines do not own the land they cultivate. Instead, land is owned by landlords, and farmers are employed by them. The Philippines received this hacienda culture from the Spanish.

While in the Middle age, people were given land in exchange for their labor during the feudal system. From the top, where the king gave a baron land in exchange for warriors, it descended to the bottom, where a peasant received land in exchange for cultivating crops. The mansion served as the hub of life during that era. The local lord was in charge of the manor.

The political-economic system of feudalism in Western Europe produced a social structure with concomitant military responsibilities. It gave rise to a code of conduct known as chivalry, as well as a beautiful genre of literature that assisted Europeans in preserving and cultivating a sense of pride in their pasts.

Feudalism significantly impacted medieval life in two ways. Feudalism hindered the development of a cohesive administration as well as trade and the economy.

3. “Did the policies implemented during the time of Feudal Japan lead to their rise or decline?" feudalism


Answer:

Japan’s Tokugawa (or Edo) period, which lasted from 1603 to 1867, would be the final era of traditional Japanese government, culture and society before the Meiji Restoration of 1868 toppled the long-reigning Tokugawa shoguns and propelled the country into the modern era. Tokugawa Ieyasu’s dynasty of shoguns presided over 250 years of peace and prosperity in Japan, including the rise of a new merchant class and increasing urbanization. To guard against external influence, they also worked to close off Japanese society from Westernizing influences, particularly Christianity. But with the Tokugawa shogunate growing increasingly weak by the mid-19th century, two powerful clans joined forces in early 1868 to seize power as part of an “imperial restoration” named for Emperor Meiji. The Meiji Restoration spelled the beginning of the end for feudalism in Japan, and would lead to the emergence of modern Japanese culture, politics and society.

26. 1. What are your insights on the impact of feudal system in the contemporary Philippines?2. How feudal system during contributes to the public services in the country? 3. Discuss the definition of Poverty by ADB and relate in the present time. ​


Answer:

The feudal system, which was prevalent during the Spanish colonial period in the Philippines, has had a lasting impact on the country's social and economic structures. The legacy of feudalism is still visible today in the Philippines, particularly in the way that wealth and power are concentrated in the hands of a small elite, while the majority of the population remains poor and marginalized. This has led to social inequality, political corruption, and a lack of access to basic services such as education, healthcare, and housing. The influence of feudalism on Philippine society has also contributed to the persistence of authoritarianism and political dynasties, which hinder the development of a truly democratic and inclusive political system.

The feudal system in the Philippines has had a negative impact on public services, as it has allowed those in power to use public resources for their own benefit, rather than for the benefit of the people. The concentration of power in the hands of a small elite has resulted in a lack of accountability and transparency, leading to the mismanagement of public funds and the failure to provide basic services to those who need them most. Political dynasties and patronage networks have also contributed to the inefficient delivery of public services, as those in power often prioritize their own interests over the needs of the people.

The Asian Development Bank (ADB) defines poverty as the lack of access to basic needs such as food, shelter, healthcare, and education. In the present time, poverty remains a major issue in many countries, including the Philippines. Despite recent economic growth, poverty rates in the Philippines remain high, with more than 16% of the population living below the poverty line. This is due in part to the unequal distribution of wealth and power in the country, which has resulted in a lack of opportunities for many people to improve their lives. The COVID-19 pandemic has also exacerbated poverty in the Philippines, as many people have lost their jobs and struggled to access basic necessities. To address poverty in the Philippines, it is necessary to address the root causes of inequality and create policies and programs that prioritize the needs of the most vulnerable members of society.
