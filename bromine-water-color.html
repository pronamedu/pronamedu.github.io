1. observation of bromine water​


Answer:

Bromine water is an orange solution of bromine. It becomes colourless when it is shaken with an alkene. Alkenes can decolourise bromine water, but alkanes cannot. ... This has the effect of 'saturating' the molecule, and will turn an alkene into an alkane.

Answer:

brown

A solution containing bromine will be added to the compounds being tested. The bromine has an orange or brown color. If this color disappears, it means that the bromine is getting used up and therefore the compound is reacting with the bromine. Thus, disappearance of the orange color corresponds to a positive reaction.

17. What type of chemical bond is expected between bromine and bromine?


Between bromine and bromine covalent bonds will occur by using shared electron pairs

Further explanation

Elements based on electrical conductivity can be divided into two elements, namely metal elements (conductors) and non-metals (non-conductors / insulators)

In modern periodic systems, the metal element is generally located on the left while the non-metal is on the right.

The formation of electron configurations such as noble gases can be done by sharing electrons with covalent bonds or hand over electrons with ionic bonds

1. ionic bonding

Bonding that occurs due to electron handover. Can occur in metal and non-metal atoms. To get stability, atoms release or bind electrons to get stable electron regulation from noble gases

2. covalent bonding

Bonding that occurs due to shared use of electron pairs

The two bound atoms contribute their electrons to produce bonds

Generally occurs in non-metallic elements

Unstable atomic atoms will try to form stable electron configurations like those of noble gases. Where noble gases have the number of outer electrons (valence) 2 or 8

The small difference in electronegativity between two nonmetal elements results in a non-polar covalent bond. Whereas the greater electronegativity difference makes the compound ionic

If the electronegativity difference between the atoms is greater than 1.7, the compounds formed are ionic.

Generally, atoms with high ionisation energies (difficult to release electrons -> difficult to form positive ions) or atoms with low affinity (difficult to attract electrons -> difficult to form negative ions) will form electron pairs that are shared in the form of covalent bonds. The electron pair formed in a bond can originate from one atom or from 2 binding atoms

The Br atom is an atom located in group 7A and period 4, and belongs to a nonmetal atom.

The atomic number of Br is 35, so it takes 1 electron to be stable like the electron configuration of the Kr atom (noble gas) which has atomic number 36.

Br atoms have a large ionisation energy so that it will be difficult to release electrons, so that the bound Br atoms will use each other together with their electrons and form covalent bonds of Br₂

lipids are a class of biological molecules that are insoluble in water and soluble

in nonpolar solvents. There are many different categories of lipids and each category has different components present in its structure.

Fatty acids are components of many types of lipids. Fatty acids are carboxylic

acids with very long hydrocarbon chains, usually 12-18 carbon atoms long. Even though

these carboxylic acids can hydrogen bond with water, they are insoluble because of the length of their hydrocarbon chains. Fatty acids can be saturated or unsaturated. A

saturated fatty acid contains no carbon-carbon double bonds, so it is “saturated” with

hydrogen. Unsaturated fatty acids contain one or more cis double bonds. (Very few

naturally occurring fatty acids contain trans double bonds.) The presence of cis double

bonds has an important effect on the melting point of the fatty acid.

27. For detecting proteins a suitable reagent in the following is A) iodine solution B) bromine water C) alkaline copper sulfate solution D) a piece of paper


C. Alkaline copper sulfate solution

Different kinds of tests are being used to determine the presence of certain elements and molecules. When these molecules and/or elements react with the chemicals and solutions used in the test, they yield positive and negative results to confirm the presence and/or absence of the elements and molecules. One of the common molecules being tested are proteins. Proteins are macromolecules and one of the biomolecules found in all living things that are vital in structural form, enzymatic and hormonal activities. The common test performed to determine the presence of proteins in a sample is called Biuret Test, wherein a solution of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and copper II (Cu2+) sulfate (CuSO4) is used. A positive result for the presence of proteins in a sample is when a change in color, particularly from blue to purple, is observed. The purple complex formed is called the chelate complex. It happens due to the reaction between the copper molecules and the peptide bonds (2 or more bonds) present in the sample. The copper ion attracts the electrons of the nitrogen molecules present in the protein, making the complex possible. The darker the shade of purple observed, the more peptide bonds or the larger the protein is present in the sample. The lighter the purple shade, the lesser the peptide bonds or the smaller the protein present in the sample. This is best observed when albumin or egg white is used in the experiment (positive control). In some cases, a pink shade is observed as a result. This means that there are amino acids (protein’s building blocks) or single-bonded peptide bond present in the sample, making it a negative result. Another negative result is when there is no change of color happening. This just means that no protein is present in the sample. This negative control is best observed when de-ionized water is used or food samples with no proteins on it are used.
