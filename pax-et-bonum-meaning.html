The answer is  eudaimonia.

In Aristotelian ethics, eudaimonia (sometimes spelled eudaemonia) refers to the state of human flourishing or well-being. The prevalent English translation of the ancient Greek term "happiness" is unfortunate because eudaimonia, as interpreted by Aristotle and most other ancient philosophers, does not imply a state of mind or a sensation of pleasure or contentment, as "happiness" indicates. Eudaimonia is the highest human good, according to Aristotle, and the only human good that is desired for its own sake (rather than for the purpose of something else) (as a means toward some other end).

Every living or human-made creature, including its parts, has a unique or characteristic purpose or activity that separates it from everything else, according to Aristotle. The highest good of a thing is its ability to perform its characteristic function well, and the virtue or excellence of a thing is whatever traits or attributes enable it to do so successfully. (Thus, a knife's virtue or excellence is whatever enables good cutting performance, an eye's virtue or excellence is whatever enables good seeing performance, and so on.)
