4. what is the definition of yoyo dieting


Yo-yo dieting or yo-yo effect, also known as weight cycling, is a term coined by Kelly D. Brownell at Yale University, in reference to the cyclical loss and gain of weight, resembling the up-down motion of a yo-yo. In this process, the dieter is initially successful in the pursuit of weight loss but is unsuccessful in maintaining the loss long-term and begins to gain the weight back. The dieter then seeks to lose the regained weight, and the cycle begins again.

14. What is the total mechanical energy of a yoyo


The total mechanical energy of an object refers to the sum of its kinetic and potential energies.

Before the yo-yo starts moving, it possesses energy in the form of gravitational potential energy. Its amount is equivalent to the product of its mass, its height from the ground and the acceleration due to gravity (-9.8 m/s²).

Upon moving or as the yo-yo starts to fall, its gravitational potential energy decreases and transforms to kinetic energy. The amount of its kinetic energy is equivalent to the half the product of its mass and the square of its velocity. It increases as the gravitational potential energy decreases.

Take note that the total mechanical energy of the yo-yo is conserved, which means it is constant all throughout. The gravitational potential energy and the kinetic energy will constantly change, depending on the position of the yo-yo from the ground (height) and its velocity. However, the total mechanical energy will not change.

20. what is the rules of the game yoyo​


Explanation:

A yo-yo (also spelled yoyo) is a toy consisting of an axle connected to two disks, and a string looped around the axle, similar to a spool. It is played by holding the free end of the string known as the handle (by inserting one finger—usually the middle or index finger—into a slip knot), allowing gravity (or the force of a throw and gravity) to spin the yo-yo and unwind the string (similar to how a pullstring works). The player then allows the yo-yo to wind itself back to the player's hand, exploiting its spin (and the associated rotational energy). This is often called "yo-yoing".
