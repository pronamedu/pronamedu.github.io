15. For triangle ABC, if A=50 deg, B=30 deg, and a=12 m, what is the measure of side c?​


✏️LAW OF SINES==============================

[tex] \large \bold{\blue{PROBLEM:}} [/tex] For triangle ABC, if A=50 deg, B=30 deg, and a=12 m, what is the measure of side c?

[tex] \large \bold{\blue{SOLUTION:}} [/tex] The given oblique triangle ABC had a given of two angles and a side opposite to one given angle (AAS), this can be solved using the law of sines.

» The law of sines can be solved when an angle and it's opposite side is given, a missing angle and it's opposite side is given and vice versa. Solving for side (c) needs the measure of it's opposite angle (∠C). Find angle C first and then the side c.

• Solve for angle C

[tex] \sf \angle A + \angle B + \angle C = 180 \degree [/tex]

[tex] \sf 50 \degree + 30 \degree + \angle C = 180 \degree [/tex]

[tex] \sf 80 \degree + \angle C = 180 \degree [/tex]

[tex] \sf \angle C = 180 \degree - 80 \degree [/tex]

[tex] \sf \angle C = 100 \degree [/tex]

• Solve for side (c)

[tex] \sf \frac{sin \: A}{a} = \frac{sin \: C}{c} \\ [/tex]

[tex] \sf \frac{sin \: 50 \degree}{12m} = \frac{sin \: 100 \degree}{c} \\ [/tex]

[tex] \sf c(sin \: 50 \degree) = 12m(sin \: 100 \degree) [/tex]

[tex] \sf c(sin \: 50 \degree) = 11.82m [/tex]

[tex] \sf \frac{c(\cancel{sin \: 50 \degree})}{\cancel{sin \: 50 \degree}} = \frac{11.82m}{sin \: 50 \degree} \\ [/tex]

[tex] \sf c = 15.43m [/tex]

[tex] \large \therefore \underline{\boxed{\tt \purple{Side \: c \: is \: 15.43 \: meters}}} [/tex]

==============================

19. QUESTION 11 A beam of light is incident on a liquid of 1.40 refractive index. The reflected rays are completely polarized. What is the angle of refraction of the beam? A. 40 deg B. 35 deg C. 31 deg D. 39 deg​


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: Calculate the Brewster's (polarizing) angle.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \theta_{p} & = \text{arctan} \: \mu \\ & = \text{arctan} \: 1.40 \\ & = 54.46^{\circ} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Final Step: Calculate the angle of refraction.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \theta_{2} & = 90^{\circ} - \theta_{p} \\ & = 90^{\circ} - 54.46^{\circ} \\ & = \boxed{35.54^{\circ}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the answer is B. 35 deg.

22. What is the exact value of 2sin 30 deg * cos 30 deg​


We know that sin 30 degrees = 1/2 and cos 30 degrees = √3/2. Substituting these values, we get:

2sin 30 degrees * cos 30 degrees

= 2 * (1/2) * (√3/2)

= √3

Therefore, the exact value of 2sin 30 degrees * cos 30 degrees is √3.

28. The outer of a building is constructed by a concrete blocks. If the wall is 10m long at 20 deg C, how much longer is the wall on the hot day (49 deg C)? How much shorter is it in a cold day (10 deg C)​.


Answer: The wall is approximately 3.48 millimeters longer on a hot day. In a cold day, the wall is approximately 1.2 millimeters shorter.

Step-by-step explanation:

We can use the coefficient of linear expansion of concrete to determine the change in length of the wall due to temperature changes. The formula for the change in length is given by:

[tex]\sf\qquad\dashrightarrow \Delta L = L0 * \alpha * \Delta T[/tex]

where:

ΔL is the change in lengthL0 is the initial length at temperature T0α is the coefficient of linear expansionΔT is the change in temperature

For concrete, α is approximately 1.2 x 10^-5 /°C.

On a hot day, the temperature change is:

[tex]\sf:\implies \Delta T_1 = 49^{\circ}C - 20^{\circ}C = 29^{\circ}C[/tex]

The change in length is:

[tex]\sf:\implies \Delta L_1 = 10\: m * 1.2 \times 10^{-5} /^{\circ}C * 29^{\circ}C[/tex]

[tex]\sf:\implies \boxed{\bold{\:\:\Delta L_1 = 0.00348\: m\:\:}}\:\:\:\green{\checkmark}[/tex]

Therefore, the wall is approximately 3.48 millimeters longer on a hot day.

On a cold day, the temperature change is:

[tex]\sf:\implies \Delta T_2 = 10^{\circ}C - 20^{\circ}C = -10^{\circ}C[/tex]

The change in length is:

[tex]\sf:\implies \Delta L_2 = 10\: m * 1.2 \times 10^-5 /^{\circ}C * (-10^{\circ}C)[/tex]

[tex]\sf:\implies \boxed{\bold{\:\:\Delta L_2 = -0.0012\: m\:\:}}\:\:\:\green{\checkmark}[/tex]

Therefore, the wall is approximately 1.2 millimeters shorter on a cold day.

30. Given: F1 = 20 N, 25 deg F2 = 35 N, 120 deg. Find the magnitude and direction of the resultant force. scale: 5 N = 1 cm.a. 68.77 N 89.07 degb. 28.77 N 59.07 degc. 38. 77 N 89.07 deg​


The magnitude and direction of the resultant force : c. 38. 77 N ,89.07°

Further explanation

Each vector can be described in two perpendicular vectors  

The first vector is a vector component on the x-axis (Fx) and the second vector is a vector component on the y-axis (Fy)  

Using the principle rules of cosines and sines in triangles then:  

Fx = F cos θ  

Fy = F sin θ  

Known

[tex]\tt F_1=20~N, \theta=25^o\\\\F_2=35~N,\theta=120^o[/tex]

[tex]\tt F_{1x}=20~cos~25=18.126\\\\F_{1y}=20~sin~25=8.452\\\\F_{2x}=35~cos~120=-17.5\\\\F_{2y}=35~sin~120=30.310\\\\\sum F_x=F_{1x}+F_{2x}\\\\\sum F_x=18.126-17.5=\boxed{\bold{0.626}}\\\\\sum F_y=F_{1y}+F_{2y}\\\\\sum F_y=8.452+30.310=\boxed{\bold{38.762}}[/tex]

The resultant force :

[tex]\tt F=\sqrt{F_x^2+F_y^2}\\\\F=\sqrt{0.626^2+38.762^2}\\\\F=\boxed{\bold{38.767~N}}[/tex]

The direction :

[tex]\tt tan~\theta=\dfrac{\sum F_y}{\sum F_x}\\\\tan~\theta=\dfrac{38.762}{0.626}\\\\\theta=\boxed{\bold{89.075}}[/tex]
