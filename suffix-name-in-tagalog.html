7. what suffixes should you use in writing the names of polyatomic ions that finish in ITE?​


Answer:

When writing the names of polyatomic ions that end in "ite," the suffixes you should use will depend on whether the ion is an anion (negatively charged) or cation (positively charged).

For anions, you would typically replace "ite" with "ate" to indicate the presence of one additional oxygen atom. For example:

Nitrite ion (NO2-) becomes Nitrate ion (NO3-)

Sulfite ion (SO3^2-) becomes Sulfate ion (SO4^2-)

For cations, you would typically add the word "ion" to the end of the name. For example:

Ferrite ion (FeO2^2+) becomes Ferrite ion (FeO2^2+ ion)

Cobaltite ion (CoAsS) becomes Cobaltite ion (CoAsS ion)

It's important to note that these naming conventions may not apply to every polyatomic ion that ends in "ite," so it's always a good idea to consult a reliable source or reference material for specific naming rules.

30. What is the suffix used in naming an ketone ​


The common names for ketones are formed by naming both alkyl groups attached to the carbonyl then adding the suffix -ketone. The attached alkyl groups are arranged in the name alphabetically. Ketones take their name from their parent alkane chains. The ending -e is removed and replaced with -one.
